The Situation today

In the last decade, China has adopted a slower-growing economic model that focuses more on private consumption and innovation rather than fixed investments and exports. This shift is necessary due to the "Middle Income Trap," where countries with strong economic growth eventually lose momentum because they fail to adopt new sources of growth, such as innovations.

Their plan "Made in China 2025," announced in 2015, is a major priority. The plan aims to modernize China's manufacturing in 10 key sectors with extensive government support, intending to position China as a significant global player in these sectors. However, there are concerns that their intention may be to exclude foreign companies and potentially dominate the global market.
China, of course, also faces some economic problems. The lack of enforcement of regulations leads to corruption in the government and misguided investments. In China, success is more closely tied to connections with the government than to the natural movements of the market. Foreign companies also struggle to negotiate with China, as regulations are inconsistent and opaque. Additionally, contracts are often not honored, and rights are not protected, partly due to the absence of an independent judiciary.
Since Xi Jinping took power in 2012, he has implemented many anti-corruption measures, but it is suspected that these also serve to eliminate political opponents. Apparently, this has a negative effect on the economy, as government officials fear undertaking projects if they believe they could be criticized by the central government. Observers argue that without greater transparency in the government, significant progress in the fight against corruption is unlikely to be achieved.
